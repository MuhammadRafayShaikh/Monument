Ancient World Wonders

The creation of the first list of the Wonders of the world is attributed to Herodotus (famous Greek historian, 484 BC – 425 BC) and Callimachus of Cyrene (chief of the Library of Alexandria 305 – 240 BC). However, it is also believed that the traditional list was also made by Philo of Byzantium and written on his work "On the Seven Wonders" in 225 BC. Finally, around 140 BC, Antipater of Sidon compiled a later version of the list describing the structures in a poem. The monuments mentioned in these lists were inspired by the mythology, religion and art of the ancient great civilizations of the world. The Seven Wonders of the Ancient World reflected the ability of the men to change the nature in order to build wonderful and beautiful structures which amaze and inspire the people. It believes that these constructions of classical antiquity were constructed since 2700 B.C. but, unfortunately, only one of the wonders mentioned by Herodotus has survived until today: The Pyramids of Giza. Statue of Zeus

The Statue of Zeus at Olympia was a colossal statue located in the Temple of Zeus in Olympia, Greece. Created by the renowned sculptor Phidias around 430 BC, this statue was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. The statue was approximately 40 feet tall and depicted the Greek god Zeus seated on a throne. It was made primarily of ivory plates and gold panels over a wooden framework. The detailed craftsmanship and grandeur of the statue were legendary, showcasing the artistic excellence of ancient Greece. The Temple of Zeus at Olympia was constructed specifically to house this monumental statue, and it was revered as a symbol of power and divinity. It became a focal point of the Olympic Games, which were held nearby. The statue's presence added a sense of awe and spiritual significance to the games, which were dedicated to Zeus.

The Colossus of Rhodes

The Colossus of Rhodes was a monumental statue of the Greek sun god Helios, erected on the island of Rhodes in Greece. Created by the sculptor Chares of Lindos, it stood near the harbor entrance, welcoming sailors to the island. This statue was one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. Constructed between 292 and 280 BC, the Colossus of Rhodes was a marvel of ancient engineering. It was made primarily of bronze and iron framework, standing over 30 meters (around 100 feet) tall, making it one of the tallest statues in the ancient world. Legend has it that the statue stood with its legs apart, allowing ships to pass beneath it, although this depiction is disputed among historians. Regardless, it was an impressive feat of craftsmanship and engineering for its time. Tragically, an earthquake struck Rhodes in 226 BC, causing significant damage to the statue. It is said that the Colossus of Rhodes stood for around 56 years before its collapse. The city of Rhodes lacked the resources and means to repair the colossal structure, so it remained in ruins for centuries.
